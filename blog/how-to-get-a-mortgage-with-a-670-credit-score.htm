How to Get a Mortgage with a 670 Credit Score

  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Blog Details

Let's be real. A 670 credit score feels like a financial purgatory. It’s not terrible, but it’s not great. You’ve managed your finances well enough to avoid the deep red zones, yet you’re constantly reminded that you’re just shy of that coveted "good" credit tier. In the calm waters of a stable economy, this score might have been a minor hurdle. But today? With soaring inflation, volatile interest rates, and a housing market that feels like a rollercoaster, that 670 number can feel like a thousand-pound weight on your homeownership dreams.

The good news is this: a 670 credit score is absolutely mortgage-worthy. It sits right at the cusp of what most lenders consider the minimum for "prime" loans, distancing you clearly from the subprime category. The path to approval isn't about magic; it's about strategy, preparation, and understanding the new rules of the game in a post-pandemic financial landscape. This isn't just about getting a loan; it's about securing a financially sustainable future for you and your family in an uncertain world.

Understanding Your 670 Credit Score in the Lender's Eyes

First, let's decode what your score really means. A 670 FICO® Score is generally considered "Fair." You’re in a zone where lenders will approve you, but they see you as a slightly higher risk than someone with a 740 or above. This perception is the single most important factor that will shape your mortgage journey.

Why Lenders Pause (Slightly) at 670

Lenders are in the business of risk management. Your credit score is a quick, numerical summary of your history of handling debt. With a 670, the data suggests you might have had a few missteps—perhaps a late payment or two, a high credit card balance, or a collection account that’s now resolved. In an era of economic uncertainty, where job stability can be shaky and household budgets are stretched thin by inflation, lenders are scrutinizing these profiles more carefully. They need to be confident you can withstand economic shocks, like a sudden jump in your adjustable mortgage rate or a surprise car repair, without defaulting on your largest debt.

The Silver Lining: You're in the Game

Do not despair. A 670 is above the minimum threshold for the most popular and accessible mortgage programs in the United States. This is your key takeaway. You are not relegated to exotic or predatory loan products. You are squarely in the mainstream mortgage market, albeit with slightly less favorable terms than your higher-scoring friends.

Your Game Plan: Securing a Mortgage with a 670 Score

Getting a mortgage with a 670 requires a proactive, organized, and patient approach. It’s not about finding one magic bullet, but about presenting the most compelling, holistic financial picture possible to a lender.

1. Choose the Right Mortgage Loan Type

Your choice of loan program is your first and most powerful strategic decision. Some programs are far more forgiving of a fair credit score than others.

  • FHA Loans (Federal Housing Administration): This is often the superstar for borrowers with credit scores in the 670 range. FHA loans are government-insured, which protects the lender if you default. Because of this backing, lenders can offer more flexible terms. The minimum score can be as low as 580 with a 3.5% down payment, but with a 670, you’re in a strong position to qualify. Be aware of the mandatory Mortgage Insurance Premium (MIP), which includes an upfront fee and an annual premium for the life of the loan in most cases.
  • Conventional Loans (Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac): Yes, you can get a conventional loan with a 670! The official minimum is often 620, but a 670 will get you in the door. The catch? Your interest rate will be higher, and your private mortgage insurance (PMI) will be more expensive than for someone with a 740+ score. However, with a strong overall application, this is a viable path.
  • VA Loans (Department of Veterans Affairs): If you are a qualified veteran, active-duty service member, or surviving spouse, this is your best option. VA loans have no official minimum credit score, and many lenders are comfortable with a 670. They also require no down payment and no monthly mortgage insurance, making them an incredibly powerful benefit.
  • USDA Loans (U.S. Department of Agriculture): For eligible homes in designated rural and suburban areas, USDA loans offer 100% financing. While they don't have a hard minimum, a 670 is typically considered a solid score for approval.

2. Save for a Larger Down Payment

In a high-interest-rate environment, your down payment is your best tool to offset a lower credit score. Think of it as a "credibility deposit." A larger down payment immediately reduces the lender's risk.

If you can move from a 3% down payment to a 10% or even 20% down payment, you accomplish several things: - You lower the loan-to-value (LTV) ratio, making the loan less risky. - You might qualify for a better interest rate. - You will borrow less money, leading to lower monthly payments—a critical advantage when dealing with inflation and high costs for other essentials like food and energy. - For conventional loans, a larger down payment can lead to lower PMI costs or help you cancel it sooner.

3. Get Your Financial Documents in Impeccable Order

Since your credit score won't do all the talking, your other documents need to speak volumes. Lenders will perform a deep dive into your finances, a process that has become more rigorous since the 2008 financial crisis and subsequent economic upheavals.

  • Stable Employment: Lenders love consistency. They typically want to see a two-year history in the same field of work. In today's gig economy, if you're a freelancer or contractor, be prepared with two years of tax returns to prove stable income.
  • Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI): This is a huge one. Your DTI is your total monthly debt payments divided by your gross monthly income. There are two types: a front-end ratio (housing costs only) and a back-end ratio (all recurring debts). With a 670 score, you'll want your back-end DTI to be well below 43%, and ideally closer to 36%. Pay down credit card balances and avoid taking on new debt (like a car loan) before applying for a mortgage.
  • Cash Reserves: Having several months of mortgage payments (including taxes and insurance) saved in your account after closing is a massive green flag for lenders. It shows you can handle unexpected financial setbacks, a trait highly valued in volatile economic times.

4. Shop Around for Lenders—Aggressively

Do not, under any circumstances, accept the first offer you get. Different lenders have different "risk-based pricing" models. One lender might see your 670 and offer a 7.5% interest rate, while another, who specializes in working with borrowers in your situation, might offer 7.0%. That half-point difference can save you tens of thousands of dollars over the life of the loan.

Get quotes from at least three to five different sources: - A large national bank - A local credit union (they often have more flexibility) - An online mortgage lender - An independent mortgage broker (who can shop your application to multiple lenders)

Navigating the Modern Obstacles: Inflation and Rising Rates

The ghost of 2021's 3% mortgages is long gone. Today's market is defined by the Federal Reserve's fight against inflation, which has led to the highest mortgage rates in over two decades. This new reality directly impacts your strategy with a 670 score.

How High Rates Affect Your Buying Power

The primary challenge is affordability. The same monthly payment that bought a $400,000 house at 3% might only qualify you for a $280,000 house at 7.5%. This forces a difficult conversation about your budget and your must-haves in a home. You may need to adjust your expectations, consider different neighborhoods, or look for homes that need some cosmetic work.

Using Your Mortgage as an Inflation Hedge

While it sounds counterintuitive, a fixed-rate mortgage can be a powerful tool against long-term inflation. You are locking in your largest housing expense for 30 years. As rents and the cost of everything else continue to rise with inflation, your principal and interest payment remains unchanged. This provides incredible long-term stability. The short-term pain of a higher rate can lead to long-term gain.

Actionable Steps to Strengthen Your Application Today

While you're shopping for a home and talking to lenders, you can take immediate steps to potentially improve your standing.

  • Dispute Credit Report Errors: Get your free reports from AnnualCreditReport.com and scrutinize them. A single error, like an account that isn't yours or an incorrect late payment, could be dragging your score down. Disputing and removing it could push you into the 700s.
  • Become an Authorized User: If you have a family member with a long-standing, high-limit, and perfectly paid credit card, ask if they will add you as an authorized user. You don't even need the card. Their positive payment history can be imported onto your credit report, giving your score a quick boost.
  • Pay Down Revolving Balances: The single fastest way to improve a credit score is to lower your credit utilization ratio—the amount of credit you're using compared to your limits. Aim to get your card balances below 30% of their limits, and ideally below 10%. This alone could lift a 670 into the 690s within a billing cycle or two.
  • Do Not Close Old Accounts: The length of your credit history matters. Closing an old, paid-off credit card shortens your average account age and can hurt your score. Keep them open, even if you don't use them.

The journey to homeownership with a 670 credit score is a marathon, not a sprint. It demands diligence, financial discipline, and a clear-eyed view of the current economic landscape. But by leveraging the right loan programs, presenting a rock-solid financial profile, and shopping strategically, you can turn the key to your new home and secure a piece of stability in an unpredictable world. Your 670 score is not a stop sign; it's a yield sign, prompting you to proceed with a smart, well-informed strategy.
